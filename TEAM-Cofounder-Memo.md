w# TEAM Product Opportunity: Cofounder Memo

Date: April 7, 2026

## Purpose

This memo summarizes my view of the TEAM software opportunity after reviewing the strategy report. It is meant to help us decide whether this is a real company, what the best wedge is, how to validate demand with hospitals, and what an MVP should actually include.

## Bottom Line

The idea is strong if we treat it as a focused reimbursement-risk software company first, not as a full hospital operations platform on day one.

The best wedge is a CFO-facing TEAM financial simulator that helps hospitals understand:
- expected exposure under TEAM
- the biggest drivers of reconciliation outcomes
- what operational changes are worth pursuing

The broader vision, episode command center plus discharge optimization, is directionally right but much harder. It requires deeper data integration, clinical workflow adoption, and organizational trust. If we try to build all of that too early, execution risk will likely outrun demand.

## Why This Opportunity Is Real

TEAM creates unusual urgency because:
- participation is mandatory for a defined set of hospitals
- downside risk becomes real quickly
- financial impact is measurable
- hospitals need to act before CMS reconciliation tells them whether they won or lost

That combination is rare. This is not a generic workflow pitch. It is a response to a concrete reimbursement change with an identifiable buyer and budget owner.

## What Is Most Compelling

The strongest part of the strategy is the sequencing:
- land with finance
- prove value with exposure modeling and scenario planning
- expand into operations once the economic story is trusted

That is much more credible than leading with an AI discharge tool or a broad care coordination platform.

The most promising initial buyer is:
- CFO
- VP Finance
- in some cases a VP Population Health, VP Value-Based Care, or bundled payment lead

The reason is simple: these people already own the financial risk and can justify budget based on downside protection or savings capture.

## What Concerns Me

### 1. The report understates competition

There may be no exact product with this exact packaging, but hospitals do not buy based on product taxonomy. We are competing with:
- internal finance and analytics teams
- consultants advising on TEAM readiness
- existing value-based care vendors
- EHR reporting
- fragmented tools that are "good enough"

The real question is not whether there is a direct competitor. It is whether we can provide enough speed, clarity, and decision support that a hospital will pay rather than stitch together internal workarounds.

### 2. The roadmap spans three different businesses

The proposed modules are not just features. They are different product motions:
- Module 1: finance analytics software
- Module 2: operational monitoring software
- Module 3: clinical-adjacent decision support

Each one has different buyers, implementation requirements, trust thresholds, and adoption risks.

### 3. The discharge optimizer is high value but high risk

A tool that influences discharge destination is touching:
- case manager workflow
- physician preferences
- patient choice requirements
- readmission risk
- perceived liability

That is not impossible, but it is much harder than a finance simulator. We should earn the right to build it after proving we can get trusted data and trusted outputs in front of finance and operational leaders.

### 4. Integration complexity is likely the real bottleneck

The report is directionally right that integration will matter, but in practice this may be the main determinant of success. To be useful, we need enough timely data to estimate:
- episode attribution
- current care setting
- expected target price or target range
- discharge pattern
- basic quality or readmission risk indicators

If implementation is painful, hospitals will delay or shrink the purchase.

## Investor-Style View

This looks like a credible thesis, but not yet a complete venture case.

What makes it attractive:
- mandatory CMS model with real urgency
- a defined buyer with budget authority
- measurable ROI if we can quantify exposure and improvement levers
- potential expansion from planning to operations

What would need to be proven:
- hospitals will actually pay for the simulator before full reconciliation data is available
- implementation can be light enough to support repeatable sales
- the finance wedge can convert into broader operational adoption
- we can generate outputs hospitals cannot easily reproduce with Excel, internal BI, or consulting support

What would make this investable:
- 5 to 10 high-quality design partners
- 2 to 3 paid pilots
- evidence that buyers trust the numbers
- one repeatable ROI narrative tied to exposure reduction or savings opportunity

## Founder Recommendation

We should narrow the first product even more than the report suggests.

### Phase 1 product

Build:
- TEAM exposure assessment
- target price and scenario simulator
- basic benchmark views
- simple sensitivity analysis on discharge mix, readmissions, and quality assumptions

Do not build yet:
- full command center
- bedside workflow tooling
- prescriptive AI discharge recommendations
- multi-episode operational complexity beyond what is necessary

### Best initial market focus

Start with:
- larger mandatory TEAM hospitals
- systems with meaningful surgical volume
- likely Epic-heavy environments
- hospitals that already have a visible bundled payment or value-based care owner

Start narrow on episodes:
- LEJR first
- maybe spine second

Avoid trying to prove the product across all five episodes at launch.

## Hospital Outreach Plan

We should approach outreach as a structured design-partner and pilot sales process, not generic top-of-funnel prospecting.

### Ideal First Contacts

Primary targets:
- CFO
- VP Finance
- VP Value-Based Care
- VP Population Health
- Director of Bundled Payments
- Chief Strategy Officer in systems where finance strategy is centralized

Secondary targets for credibility and expansion:
- VP Care Management
- Chief Medical Officer
- VP Quality
- orthopedic service line leader

### Where To Find Hospitals

Prioritize:
- hospitals publicly known to be in mandatory TEAM regions
- large systems with high Medicare surgical volume
- hospitals that previously participated in BPCI-A or CJR
- hospitals presenting at HFMA, Becker's, ACMA, Vizient, Premier, or bundled payment webinars

Sources and tactics:
- CMS participant lists and public model documentation
- conference speaker rosters
- LinkedIn searches for bundled payments, finance, and value-based care leaders
- advisory firms already selling TEAM readiness work
- warm introductions from consultants, former operators, or health system executives

### Outreach Motion

Message theme:
- we help hospitals quantify TEAM exposure before CMS does
- we model which levers most affect reconciliation outcomes
- we can deliver an initial exposure assessment with relatively low lift

The first ask should be small:
- 30-minute discovery call
- or a free preliminary exposure review

The second ask:
- access to limited historical data for a design-partner assessment

The third ask:
- paid pilot for simulator access and scenario modeling support

## MVP Definition

The MVP should be a finance-first product with limited implementation burden.

### MVP Goal

Help a hospital answer:
- What is our likely TEAM exposure?
- What are the biggest cost and quality levers?
- What scenarios materially change our reconciliation outcome?

### MVP User

Primary:
- CFO
- VP Finance

Secondary:
- value-based care lead
- bundled payment program owner

### MVP Inputs

Use the lowest-friction inputs possible:
- historical claims extracts or episode-level spend files
- hospital-level volumes by episode category
- discharge destination mix
- readmission rates or proxy quality measures where available
- publicly available benchmark assumptions where hospital data is incomplete

Avoid requiring full EHR integration for MVP.

### MVP Features

1. Exposure dashboard
- estimated baseline TEAM exposure by episode category
- rough view of upside/downside range

2. Scenario modeling
- change discharge mix
- adjust readmission assumptions
- adjust quality score assumptions
- compare best case, base case, and downside scenarios

3. Episode category drill-down
- LEJR first
- optional spine second

4. Benchmarking
- compare hospital assumptions versus regional or anonymized peers where possible

5. Exportable executive summary
- board-ready PDF or slide summary
- clear financial takeaway and recommended action areas

### MVP Non-Goals

Do not include:
- bedside workflow
- real-time patient-level care coordination
- ML-heavy prediction as a core dependency
- all five episodes from day one
- full PAC provider ranking if the underlying data quality is weak

## MVP Implementation Plan

### Product Approach

Build the MVP as a services-assisted SaaS product.

That means:
- software handles the modeling and reporting
- we support onboarding manually where necessary
- we do not block launch on perfect automation

This is the right tradeoff early because the goal is learning, not infrastructure purity.

### Technical Scope

Core components:
- hospital profile and episode configuration
- scenario engine
- target price / reconciliation model
- benchmark assumptions layer
- reporting UI and export

Suggested architecture:
- simple web app for internal and pilot use
- relational database for hospital, episode, and scenario data
- ingestion layer for CSV or spreadsheet uploads
- configurable assumptions engine rather than hard-coded logic
- audit trail for scenario inputs and outputs

### Data Flow

1. Receive pilot data extract
2. Normalize episode and spend fields
3. Map into TEAM episode categories
4. Apply benchmark and target logic
5. Generate baseline exposure view
6. Allow user or analyst to run scenarios
7. Produce executive-ready output

### MVP Team

Minimum team:
- 1 product/founder lead
- 1 engineer full-time
- 1 data/analytics lead, could be founder-led initially
- part-time domain expert or advisor with bundled payment knowledge

### MVP Build Sequence

Weeks 1-2:
- define data schema
- create mock outputs
- encode first-pass TEAM financial logic

Weeks 3-4:
- build upload and normalization flow
- produce first hospital exposure output
- validate assumptions against real examples

Weeks 5-6:
- add scenario controls and benchmark comparisons
- create exportable summary output

Weeks 7-8:
- pilot with first hospital
- refine onboarding, assumptions, and reporting clarity

Weeks 9-12:
- support additional pilots
- harden data model
- identify top repeated requests for v2

## Success Metrics For MVP

Commercial:
- 5 design partners
- 2 to 3 paid pilots
- first annualized pricing signal

Product:
- time to first assessment under 2 weeks
- scenario outputs trusted by finance buyer
- repeatable onboarding with limited custom work

Strategic:
- one clear wedge use case
- evidence for the right second module
- proof that we reduce uncertainty enough to justify budget

## Recommended Positioning

We should position this as:

"TEAM financial operating software for hospitals."

Not:
- generic care coordination
- AI discharge automation
- another value-based care platform

That keeps the story sharp and matches the buyer, budget, and urgency.

## Decision Framework

We should keep working on this if, within the next 90 days, we can prove:
- finance leaders take the problem seriously
- they will pay for a simulator-driven product
- implementation can be lightweight enough to scale

We should reconsider or narrow further if:
- hospitals only want consulting, not software
- the data burden is too high for repeatable onboarding
- buyers see the product as a spreadsheet feature rather than a real system of record for planning

## Final Recommendation

This is a good opportunity if we stay disciplined.

The right move is:
- start with a narrow finance wedge
- validate willingness to pay quickly
- use services tactically
- add operational functionality only after we earn trust and understand where real workflow demand exists

If we try to build the full three-module platform now, we will likely take on too much product and implementation risk before proving that the first buyer truly exists.
